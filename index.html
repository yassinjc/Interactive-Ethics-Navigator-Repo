What is Autonomy?

Autonomy means that people have the right to make their own decisions.

In the context of ICT and research, this mainly refers to how people can choose what happens to their personal information — like their name, email, location, or opinions.

  • Things you should always ensure as a researcher:

  • Have I clearly explained what data will be collected?

  • Does the participant understand how it will be managed?

  • Can they say “no” or “opt out” without feeling pressured?

    When someone participates in a project or uses a digital product, they should always have a real choice:

  • Do I want to share this data?
  • Do I understand how it will be used?
  • Can I say “no” without pressure or negative consequences?

Why Autonomy Matters

    Respecting autonomy is not just a rule — it’s about treating people with respect.

    When people understand what’s happening and get to make their own decisions, they feel:

    • more comfortable
    • more in control
    • and more willing to participate

    Real impact of ignoring autonomy:

    • A participant may drop out of your study.
    • A user may stop using your product.
    • Someone might file a complaint or report your project.

    Small ethical mistakes can lead to bigger consequences than you might expect.

Autonomy in Action – Two Different Approaches

✅ Lisa’s Approach:

(Respecting Autonomy)

Lisa is building a feedback tool for students.

Before collecting any answers, she explains:

  • What kind of feedback will be collected
  • Why it’s being collected
  • How it will (and won’t) be used

She gives users the option to opt out, and includes a clear contact if they have questions.

Results:

  • Users feel in control
  • User participation is high
  • Users give more honest input, because they trust the process
  • Several students mention they appreciate the transparency

❌Bram's Approach:

(Ignoring Autonomy)

Bram builds a similar feedback tool, but doesn’t ask for permission.

He skips the explanation to “save time” and assumes people won’t mind.

He collects personal comments anonymously, but doesn't tell anyone how they’re stored or used.

Results:

  • Users drop out of the study or product
  • Users feel tricked and file complaints
  • Users skip questions or give false data out of distrust
  • Team loses credibility (especially in public-facing research)
  • You may violate GDPR rules → serious legal risks
